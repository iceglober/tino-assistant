# Ch. 4 — Guiding policy

Direction, not goals.

The guiding policy is the overall approach we'll take to address the crux. It isn't a target (*"grow revenue 50%"*); it's a **posture** (*"grow by deepening engagement with our top decile of users, accepting slower top-of-funnel growth"*).

A guiding policy does three things:

### 1. It names the direction

Where are we going? What's the orientation? *Toward simplicity, away from breadth. Toward depth in one segment, away from horizontal expansion. Toward a platform play, away from feature parity.* The direction is the *shape* of the response to the diagnosis.

### 2. It creates advantage

A good guiding policy positions us where our competitors aren't, or where the rules favor us, or where our existing resources have unfair leverage. If your policy could be lifted by any competitor and applied to their situation without modification, it's not creating advantage — it's a platitude.

*"We will be customer-obsessed"* — every competitor's policy.
*"We will serve mid-market healthcare buyers by leveraging our existing compliance work to undercut the enterprise vendors' implementation costs"* — a policy that only works for the company that already has the compliance work.

### 3. It names what we'll give up

This is the test most policies fail. *"We'll be the best at X"* is empty unless it implies *"so we'll be worse at Y."* A guiding policy that involves no trade-offs is a wish, not a policy.

The trade-off is uncomfortable because it commits the org. Real policy says *we'll grow slower in Europe to fund the mid-market motion,* or *we'll lose the high-touch enterprise deals to win the self-serve segment.* These commitments are costly to make and costly to walk back, which is what makes them real.

A policy without trade-offs lets every team continue doing what they were already doing under a new label. That's a rebrand, not a strategy.

## What good looks like

- A guiding policy stated in one or two sentences
- Direction + advantage + trade-off all visible
- The trade-off names something real and uncomfortable
- The policy could *not* be lifted by a competitor with no other changes

## What bad looks like

- Policy stated as a goal (*"be #1"*)
- Policy stated as a value (*"customer-obsessed"*)
- Policy without any trade-off — i.e., we'll be more of everything for everyone
- Policy generic enough to fit any company in the industry

## Diagnostic questions

- What's our guiding policy in one sentence? What direction does it set?
- What advantage does this policy create? Where does it concentrate our strength?
- What are we explicitly giving up? What will get worse as a result?
- Could this policy be copied by our competitor with no change? If yes, it isn't advantage-creating.

## Craft move

Every guiding policy must have a trade-off named in the same paragraph. If you can't say what we're giving up, the policy isn't real yet. The discomfort of the trade-off is the price of the focus the policy buys.

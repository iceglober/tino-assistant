# Ch. 2 — Diagnosis

The first leg of the kernel, and the one most strategies skip.

A diagnosis is a claim about *what's actually going on,* in concrete and falsifiable terms. It names the dominant problem and the mechanism producing it. It's specific enough to be *wrong* — which is the test that distinguishes diagnosis from description.

*"Sales are down"* isn't a diagnosis. It's a fact.
*"The market is competitive"* isn't a diagnosis. It's an observation.
*"Customers prefer simpler products"* isn't a diagnosis. It's a hypothesis.

*"Our enterprise sales motion is built for a 12-week cycle but our buyers have moved to a 3-week decision process, so our reps run out of touchpoints before the deal closes"* — *that's* a diagnosis. Specific. Mechanical. Could be wrong, and if it is, you can find out.

A real diagnosis usually has three qualities:

**1. It's mechanical.** It names what causes what. *"Trust is low"* describes a state; *"trust drops 30% on the first failed onboarding step because users have low tolerance after a paid signup"* describes a mechanism. Mechanisms are what you can act on.

**2. It rules things out.** A good diagnosis says what the problem is *not.* Not the market. Not the brand. Not pricing. By eliminating alternatives, you concentrate the analysis on what's actually load-bearing.

**3. It's specific enough to be wrong.** Vague diagnoses are politically safe because they can't be falsified. The price of safety is uselessness. Force yourself to write a diagnosis you'd be willing to bet on.

A common failure: the team produces a *list* of problems. Twelve things are wrong; we'll fix all twelve. This isn't a diagnosis — it's an inventory. Diagnosis requires ranking. Which problem, if solved, makes the others smaller or irrelevant? That's where Chapter 3 picks up.

Another failure: diagnosis as blame. *"The problem is that engineering is slow."* This sounds specific but it's actually generic — it doesn't say what's producing the slowness, and it provides no mechanism for action besides *"fire engineering."* Real diagnoses are mechanical, not personal.

## What good looks like

- A diagnosis statable in one paragraph, with cause and effect named
- Specifics that could be falsified by data
- Rules-out section: what this is *not* about
- Mechanism: how the named cause produces the observed problem

## What bad looks like

- Descriptions of state, not mechanism
- Lists of problems without ranking
- Diagnoses generic enough to apply to almost any company
- Blame-shaped diagnoses (people, not mechanisms)

## Diagnostic questions

- What's the dominant problem, in one sentence?
- What mechanism produces it? How does cause flow to effect?
- What is this *not* — what alternative diagnoses are we ruling out, and why?
- If we asked five smart outsiders to diagnose this, would they converge?

## Craft move

Write the diagnosis first, before any policy or action. Then run it through a "could-be-wrong" check: state it in a way that makes it falsifiable. If you couldn't be proven wrong, you haven't diagnosed yet — you've described.

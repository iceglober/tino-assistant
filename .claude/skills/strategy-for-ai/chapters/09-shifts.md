# Ch. 9 — Surfing the shifts

Using changes you didn't cause.

Most strategy assumes the environment is stable and the strategist is acting on it. The most powerful strategies recognize that the environment is *shifting,* and position the firm to ride the shift.

Rumelt's examples include companies that anticipated regulatory changes, demographic shifts, technology platforms, or consumer behavior changes — and positioned themselves *before* the shift made the position obvious. Netflix anticipating streaming. Apple anticipating mobile. Each was visible in retrospect; each looked uncertain at the time.

## The kinds of shifts

- **Technological** — a new capability becomes possible (LLMs, mobile, broadband, cloud)
- **Demographic** — a generational change, a population shift, an aging cohort
- **Regulatory** — laws, policies, standards, or enforcement changing
- **Cultural** — what users expect or value changing (privacy, sustainability, remote work)
- **Economic** — pricing models, financing, costs, supply structures changing
- **Competitive** — a major competitor exiting, entering, pivoting, or weakening

Most orgs miss shifts because they're focused on optimizing *within the current shape* of the environment. Their planning horizon assumes the rules don't change. The strategic move is to spend a fraction of attention on shift-detection — not as forecasting, but as **option-creation.** Build optionality early; commit later.

## Three moves that produce shift-readiness

### Notice anomalies

Things that don't fit the model are early signals of shifts. The customer using your product in an unexpected way. The competitor whose results break the pattern. The data point that "shouldn't" exist. Most orgs explain these away. Don't. (This connects directly to `systems-for-ai` Ch. 8.)

### Maintain option-creating activities

Small bets in adjacent spaces. Light investment in unproven categories. Conversations with people outside your industry. These don't all pay off, but they create the *option* to act when a shift becomes clear. The optionality is the asset; the individual bet is incidental.

### Be ready to commit hard

When a shift becomes visible, the window to act is short. Orgs that hedge — making small investments in the shift while keeping most resources on the old strategy — usually miss the window. The strategic move is to **commit early** to what becomes the new center, accepting the cost of being early-and-wrong as the price of being early-and-right.

This is also where the previous chapters land hardest. A shift creates a *new crux.* The old strategy was answering an old diagnosis; the shift changes the diagnosis. Concentration (Ch. 7) on the new crux requires *de-*concentrating from the old one, which means walking back investments and explaining the reversal. Politically expensive. Strategically necessary.

## What good looks like

- Regular attention to anomalies and shifts, not just internal metrics
- Small bets maintained in adjacent or future-relevant areas
- Willingness to make significant resource shifts when shifts become clear
- Strategic plans that name scenarios that would trigger a different strategy

## What bad looks like

- Planning that assumes the environment is static
- Anomalies routinely explained away
- Hedging that's actually fence-sitting (small bets that don't grow when conditions warrant)
- Surprise at shifts that were visible to outsiders months earlier

## Diagnostic questions

- What shifts are we tracking, and what would tell us they're real?
- Which anomalies have we noticed recently? Are we following them?
- What option-creating activities do we have running?
- If a shift became clear next month, could we move significant resources to it?

## Craft move

Build a shift-detection cadence into the strategic rhythm. Quarterly: name the shifts that might matter, the anomalies you're tracking, the options you're keeping open. This is the strategic equivalent of testing for diseases you don't have — most of the time it's wasted attention, occasionally it saves the firm.

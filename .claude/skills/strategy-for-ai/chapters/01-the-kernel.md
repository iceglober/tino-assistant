# Ch. 1 — The kernel

Most "strategy" documents are wishes with section headers. A real strategy has three things:

1. **A diagnosis** — what's actually going on, in concrete terms
2. **A guiding policy** — the overall direction we'll take in response
3. **A coherent set of actions** — specific moves that, together, advance the policy

This is Rumelt's **kernel.** It isn't a template; it's the *minimum-viable structure of a real strategy.* Most strategy documents are missing one or more of its parts — and when they are, the document is doing something else (goal-setting, vision-painting, consensus-building) under the strategy label.

The most commonly missing piece is the diagnosis. The team has goals (not strategy) and ambitions (not strategy), but no clear-eyed statement of what's actually true about their situation. Without a diagnosis, the "guiding policy" is just direction-pointing — *we'll grow! we'll innovate! we'll delight customers!* — and the "coherent actions" become a feature list.

A diagnosis is hard because it requires admitting things. It names the dominant problem and the dominant cause. It rules out other interpretations. It's specific enough to be *wrong.* Vague statements (*"the market is challenging"*) aren't diagnoses. *"Our retention is bad because our activation flow is built around a use case that's now the minority of signups"* is a diagnosis. It could be wrong, but it's specific.

The guiding policy isn't a goal. *"Increase retention 30%"* is a goal. *"Rebuild activation around the most-common current signup motivation, accepting that we'll temporarily underperform on the historical persona"* is a guiding policy. It names a direction and what we'll give up.

Coherent actions aren't a list. They're a *sequence of moves that fit together* — each one strengthening the next. If your "actions" don't depend on each other — if any could be done first, in isolation, with no loss — they're a list of features, not a coherent action plan.

## What good looks like

- All three parts named explicitly
- Diagnosis specific enough that it could be wrong
- Policy that names what we'll give up, not just what we want
- Actions sequenced; each one enabled by the previous

## What bad looks like

- "Strategy" that's mostly goals and aspirations
- No diagnosis — jumping straight to direction or action
- Actions interchangeable in order — i.e., a feature list dressed as strategy
- Three parts present in form but vague or generic in substance

## Diagnostic questions

- What's our diagnosis, in one paragraph?
- What's the guiding policy that follows? What does it forbid?
- What's the sequence of actions, and why is this order right?
- If any of these three are missing, what are we calling "strategy"?

## Craft move

Any time someone says *"the strategy is X,"* ask them to name the diagnosis. If they can't, there isn't a strategy yet — there's a wish, a direction, or a target. Useful things, all. Just not strategy.

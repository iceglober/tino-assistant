# Ch. 7 — Concentration

Most strategy succeeds or fails on a single move: did the team *concentrate,* or did they *spread*?

**Concentration** is the strategic version of leverage. The same total effort, applied in one place, produces vastly more than the same effort divided across many. Rumelt's term for the result is *pivot* — the place where concentrated effort meets a yielding obstacle.

The opposite is **spread.** Distributing effort evenly because every option seems equally meritorious. Spread is what most orgs default to, because it keeps everyone happy and offends no one. It's also why most orgs accomplish less than orgs half their size that concentrated.

## What concentration looks like

- A team that's doing one thing very seriously and many things poorly or not at all
- A budget where 70% of resources go to 20% of initiatives
- Roadmaps with one or two clear bets and a much smaller list of supporting work
- People reassigned, not added, when priorities shift
- A short list (1–3) of bets, with a longer list of work explicitly *tabled* for this cycle

## Why teams resist concentration

The politics. Concentration means telling someone their pet project is deprioritized. Telling a senior leader their domain is getting fewer resources this quarter. Saying out loud that some work won't happen at all. These conversations are uncomfortable, and *bad strategy is what you produce when you avoid them.*

Bad strategy is psychologically cheaper. Real strategy is psychologically expensive. The price difference is paid in courage, and most orgs spend it grudgingly.

## Why concentration is necessary

Because the world has **non-linearities.** The hundredth dollar of effort on the right problem is worth more than the first dollar of effort on the wrong one. Linear effort applied evenly to many problems produces linear results. Concentrated effort on the right problem produces *compounding* results.

This is also why the crux (Ch. 3) and concentration belong together. The crux tells you *where* to concentrate; concentration tells you *how hard.* A correctly identified crux with diluted resources is no better than a misidentified crux. Both fail to produce the non-linear payoff that distinguishes real strategy from busy work.

## The intersection with systems thinking

If you're using `systems-for-ai` alongside this skill: Meadows' high-leverage points (Ch. 7 of that skill) are exactly where concentration should land. Concentrating effort on a parameter is low leverage; concentrating on a goal, a rule, or a mental model is high leverage. Strategic concentration *on a high-leverage point* is what produces the lopsided wins that define real strategy.

## What good looks like

- An explicit choice of where to concentrate, named in the strategy
- Resources matching the concentration in actual budget allocation, not words
- Departments aware of what they're *not* getting this cycle and why
- A short list of bets, with a longer list of explicitly tabled work

## What bad looks like

- "Strategic priorities" numbered 1 through 8
- Every department got something they wanted
- Resource allocation roughly proportional to last cycle's
- A strategy doc that survives review without anyone losing anything

## Diagnostic questions

- Where are we concentrating? Where would the marginal next dollar go?
- Who is *not* getting what they want this cycle? Has that been said out loud?
- What would the strategy look like if we had to cut 30% of activities and double down on the rest?
- Is our concentration matched by our calendar and budget, or only by our words?

## Craft move

Every strategy presentation should include the list of things we're *not* doing. If that list is empty or short, the strategy hasn't been finished. Concentration is what the not-doing list buys.

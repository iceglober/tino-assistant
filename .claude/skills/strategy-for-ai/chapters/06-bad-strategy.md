# Ch. 6 — Bad strategy

The four hallmarks.

Rumelt's most useful negative contribution: a taxonomy of what gets called "strategy" but isn't. If you can recognize these, you can demand better — from yourself, from your team, from anyone holding a strategy doc.

### 1. Fluff

Strategy that's mostly buzzwords, big claims, and high-flown abstractions. *"We'll leverage our synergistic capabilities to deliver next-generation outcomes that drive transformational value for our stakeholders."* Strip the words and there's nothing operational. Fluff exists to *sound impressive without committing to anything.*

**Test:** Could a competitor put their logo on this document with no other changes? If yes, it's fluff.

### 2. Failure to face the challenge

A strategy that doesn't name the actual problem. The team has a crisis, but the strategy doc reads like the situation is normal. There's no diagnosis because the team can't say what's actually wrong — often because admitting it would implicate decisions or people they don't want to confront.

This is the most pernicious failure, because it's politely camouflaged. The doc looks fine. It just never says *what we're up against.*

**Test:** If the strategy doc never mentions the dominant problem the org is actually facing, it's avoidance — not strategy.

### 3. Mistaking goals for strategy

"Strategy" stated as targets. *"Grow revenue 30%. Improve retention. Become the market leader."* These are outcomes you want; they're not how you'll get them. Strategy is the *how,* and the kernel (diagnosis → policy → action) is the structure of the how.

Targets without strategy is the most common pattern in OKRs done poorly. The objectives are aspirations; the key results are metrics; nowhere in the document is there a claim about *what we'll do differently to produce those outcomes.*

**Test:** If you can replace "strategy" with "goal" in every paragraph with no change in meaning, the document is goal-setting masquerading as strategy.

### 4. Bad strategic objectives

A list of "strategic objectives" that's actually just every department's wish list relabeled. Sales wants this, marketing wants that, engineering wants the other. The "strategy" is the union of all wants. No prioritization. No trade-offs. No concentration.

Everyone's happy because everyone got their initiative on the list. Nothing gets done because no one is concentrated on anything.

**Test:** If the strategic objectives sum to more resources than the org has, the strategy hasn't been done. Real strategy concentrates; bad strategy spreads.

## Why bad strategy is so common

Real strategy is hard and uncomfortable. It requires saying no to people, naming problems out loud, accepting trade-offs, concentrating resources unevenly. Bad strategy lets you keep the team happy and the executives looking decisive while accomplishing nothing measurable.

Bad strategy is also the path of least resistance in any org with shared decision-making. Each hallmark above is the natural output of a consensus-building process where the cost of confronting one stakeholder is higher than the cost of producing a watered-down document. The watered-down document is the equilibrium.

## What good looks like

- Strategy docs free of buzzwords, with operational language
- The dominant problem named explicitly, with mechanism
- Goals separated from strategy; both present but not confused
- Concentration evident in resource allocation, not just words

## What bad looks like

- Any of the four hallmarks above
- A strategy that everyone in the room "agreed to easily"
- Documents whose key claims are unfalsifiable
- "Strategy" indistinguishable from a goals document

## Diagnostic questions

- Is this doc free of buzzwords and unfalsifiable claims?
- Does it name the actual dominant problem?
- Are goals separated from how-we-get-there?
- Does it concentrate, or does it spread?

## Craft move

When reviewing a strategy doc, run it through the four hallmarks first. Most "strategy" you encounter will fail at least two. Name which two. The next step is to ask what would change if those failures were fixed — that's where real strategy work begins.

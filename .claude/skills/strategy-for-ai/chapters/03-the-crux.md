# Ch. 3 — Finding the crux

A diagnosis isn't a list of all the problems. It's a claim about *which one matters most.*

The **crux** is Rumelt's term for the *addressable bottleneck within the broader mess.* It's the specific problem that, if solved, makes the surrounding problems easier or irrelevant. Crucially, the crux is almost never the most visible problem. It's usually one or two steps back — the underlying constraint that *produces* the visible symptoms.

Finding the crux is harder than naming a problem because it requires *triage.* Many problems are real but secondary. Many problems are unsolvable in your current resource envelope. The crux is **the biggest problem you can actually address.** Not the biggest problem (you can't always touch that). Not the easiest problem (low leverage). The biggest problem you can move.

Common failure: confusing the symptom for the crux. Retention is dropping → *"the crux is retention."* But retention is the *output.* The crux is whatever's structurally producing the retention drop — the activation problem, the wrong-customer problem, the value-delivery problem. The visible metric is the *signal,* not the *target.*

Another failure: trying to address everything. *"Our strategy is to improve the product AND expand internationally AND upgrade the brand AND re-platform engineering."* That isn't a strategy; it's a wish list. Real strategy names the crux and *concentrates* on it (see Ch. 7), accepting that the other things will likely get worse before they get better.

The crux is where systems thinking (`systems-for-ai`) and strategy meet. Meadows' leverage points hierarchy is asking the same question Rumelt's crux is asking: *where, in this complicated mess, is the place where focused effort produces disproportionate result?* Often the crux is a goal, a rule, an information flow, or a mental model — not a parameter. The strategic crux and the systems-leverage point are usually the same thing, named in different vocabularies.

## What good looks like

- A named crux that's specific, mechanically explained, and one or two steps back from the visible symptom
- Other problems acknowledged but consciously deprioritized
- Resources concentrated on the crux, not spread across all the visible problems
- A clear sense that solving the crux makes other problems smaller, not the same size

## What bad looks like

- "Crux" stated as the most visible problem (the metric, not the cause)
- Multiple "cruxes" — which means none
- Even resource distribution across many problems
- A crux that's actually unaddressable (we can't move it; calling it the crux is wishful)

## Diagnostic questions

- What's the crux here, mechanically? What produces the visible problem?
- What other problems are we explicitly *not* addressing now, and why?
- If we solved the crux, what currently-visible problems would shrink or disappear?
- Are we putting resources where the crux is, or where the visible problem is?
- Is this actually addressable for us, with our current resources?

## Craft move

State the crux in one sentence. If you can't, you haven't found it — your strategy is still in the diagnosis phase. The sentence test is brutal. Use it.

# Ch. 8 — Chain-link systems

When every link must work.

A **chain-link system** is one where overall performance is limited by the weakest link. The chain doesn't average across its links; it's only as strong as its weakest. A car needs working brakes, working steering, *and* a working engine. One failure and the whole system fails, regardless of how well the others perform.

Many products and orgs are chain-link systems. A SaaS product needs working acquisition, working activation, working retention, *and* working monetization. Strong retention can't compensate for broken activation. A startup needs working product, working go-to-market, *and* working hiring. Brilliant product can't compensate for a broken funnel. A B2B sale needs marketing, sales, customer success, and renewals all functioning — break any link and revenue stalls there.

In chain-link systems, the strategy work is to **find and fix the weakest link.** Investing in already-strong links is wasted; the bottleneck is what determines throughput.

## The trap: investing where it's exciting

Most teams invest where the energy is. The team with the loudest leader. The team with the visible wins. The team whose work is fun. None of these correlate with where the chain is weakest. If your acquisition is humming and activation is broken, every dollar spent on acquisition produces more wasted signups. The strong link can't compensate for the weak one. It can only feed it.

This is why "doing more of what's working" often fails in chain-link systems. The link that's working isn't the bottleneck. Doing more of it produces more pressure on the bottleneck, not more output.

## The advantage: chain-link is a moat

Once the chain is working — all links above threshold — chain-link domains create *defensible advantage.* Because every link is necessary, a competitor who's missing even one can't catch you, even if their other links are stronger. Excellence in a chain-link domain compounds; copying just one or two of your links isn't enough.

This is part of why the great companies seem to do everything well. They had to. The chain-link nature of their market forced them to bring every link above threshold before they could win. Now competitors look at them and see "they're good at everything" — but really they were forced into it, and now the breadth is the moat.

## The sequencing implication

In chain-link domains, the right sequence is:

1. **Identify the weakest link.** This is usually the link that's most visibly failing, but sometimes it's the link that's quietly limiting throughput before the visible failures show.
2. **Fix it to threshold.** Not best-in-class — just *working enough that another link becomes the bottleneck.*
3. **Find the new weakest link.** It's now somewhere else; the bottleneck moved.
4. **Repeat until all links are above threshold.**
5. **Only then** start investing in best-in-class on the highest-leverage link.

Most strategy work in chain-link domains is in steps 1–4. Step 5 is what creates moat, but step 5 done first produces no improvement, because the weak link is still limiting throughput.

## What good looks like

- Recognition that this is a chain-link system (or isn't)
- Diagnosis of which link is currently weakest, mechanically
- Concentrated effort on the weakest link until it crosses threshold
- Investment in best-in-class links only after all are above threshold

## What bad looks like

- Investing in the strong link because that team is exciting / vocal / well-resourced
- Treating a chain-link system as if links can average (they can't)
- Multiple simultaneous "great" initiatives while one weak link drags throughput

## Diagnostic questions

- Is this a chain-link system? What are the links?
- Which link is the bottleneck — the weakest or the most-frequently-failing?
- Where is our investment going, and does it match the bottleneck?
- What's "threshold" for each link, and are we all above it?

## Craft move

In chain-link domains, sequence the work bottleneck-first. The weakest link gets fixed before any other link gets enhanced. This often means moving resources from the loudest team to the quietest one — which is hard, and is why most chain-link orgs underperform their potential.

# Ch. 7 — Behavioral

*The pleasure in doing.*

If visceral is the first impression, **behavioral** is the second one — the one earned through actually using the thing. It's the feeling of *competence in motion.* The product responds when you expect it to. The action you took was the right one. The friction was just enough to feel intentional, never enough to feel like work.

## What behavioral pleasure responds to

- **Speed** — perceived more than actual. The Doherty Threshold (response under 400ms feels productive) is the floor. Optimistic UI is the cheat code (see Ch. 3).
- **Microinteractions** — the small motions that confirm an action landed. The button depress. The toggle slide. The checkbox tick. The heart that fills and pulses. Each one is a tiny gift.
- **Direct manipulation** — when manipulating the screen feels like manipulating the thing itself. Drag, pinch, swipe. The closer the gesture is to the action, the more pleasure in doing it.
- **Hot paths and warm paths** — the actions a user takes often should be effortless (one click, one keystroke). Actions they take rarely can be deeper (a few clicks, a confirm).
- **Keyboardability** — for power users, the difference between *great* and *love*. Every action a shortcut. Every shortcut memorable. Every shortcut consistent across the product.

## What good behavioral looks like

- Hovering reveals what's clickable; clicking confirms instantly.
- A frequently-used action is one keystroke. A rare one is properly buried.
- Microinteractions feel hand-crafted: the swipe-to-delete that springs back if you let go too early, the autocomplete that animates the selection.
- A "command bar" or quick-action pattern for power users — even if the rest of the product doesn't surface it loudly.

## What bad behavioral looks like

- Every action takes the same number of clicks regardless of how often it's used.
- Mouse-required everything. No keyboard paths.
- Stiff or absent transitions — state just changes, with no character.
- Loading on every interaction, including ones that could be optimistic.
- Microinteractions that overstay their welcome — long animations on the millionth click.

## Diagnostic questions

- Which actions do users repeat by choice? Are those effortless?
- Which actions do they avoid? Is the friction earned or accidental?
- Pick the top three actions in this product. How many keystrokes or clicks each? Could it be fewer?
- What's the character of motion here? Is it consistent across the product, or does each component animate by its own rules?

## The craft move

The actions users take a hundred times deserve the most attention. Cut a half-second off the most repeated action in your app and you've given users back hours over a year — and made the product feel measurably better, even if they couldn't tell you why.

The behavioral layer is where products are won or lost in the long term. Visceral impressions fade. Reflective meaning develops slowly. Behavioral pleasure is renewed every single use.

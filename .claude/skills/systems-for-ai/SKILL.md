# systems-for-ai

Eight chapters on how systems behave, and how to act in them with leverage. Synthesized from Donella Meadows' *Thinking in Systems* and her essay *Leverage Points: Places to Intervene in a System.*

## When to use

- Diagnosing why a product, team, or organization behaves the way it does
- When a metric won't move despite real effort
- When fixes produce the opposite of what you wanted
- Choosing where to intervene when many options look plausible
- Designing feedback loops into a product, process, or org
- Resisting the urge to chase symptoms in any complex situation

## Modes

Pick one before starting. The chapters are the same in both; the workflow over them differs.

**AUDIT** — when a situation exists and is behaving in a way you want to understand or change (a stuck metric, a recurring problem, a team dynamic, a market move). Walk each chapter's diagnostic questions against the situation. Produce a structured report:

- *Symptom* — what's visible, the bump everyone sees
- *Structure* — what stocks, flows, loops, and delays produce the symptom (Ch. 1–5)
- *Mental models* — what beliefs in the system keep the structure in place (Ch. 6)
- *Leverage* — where to intervene, ranked by structural power (Ch. 7)
- *Intervention plan* — what to do, what to expect, what to watch for

The report must distinguish symptoms from structure. If it only describes what's visible, the diagnosis isn't done.

**BUILD** — when designing a new system or modifying one (a process, a product loop, a team structure, a metric). Walk the chapters as design questions: name the stocks, flows, loops, and delays before designing interventions; surface the mental models users will bring; decide where you want leverage to live and where you deliberately *don't.* Output shape: system design with explicit loops and intended leverage.

If neither mode fits, default to AUDIT — seeing is the first move whether or not you'll intervene.

## Output discipline

In both modes, every diagnosis or design should cite the chapter that justifies it. A claim about "the system" without naming the loop, stock, or delay is vibes — and vibes are what most "let's fix X" conversations already are.

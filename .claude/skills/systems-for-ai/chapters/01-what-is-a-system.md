# Ch. 1 — What is a system

A system is three things: **elements**, **interconnections**, and a **purpose**. The elements are usually the most visible. The purpose is usually the most invisible. And the interconnections — how the elements relate — are where most of the behavior lives.

Most "explanations" of why something behaves the way it does name the elements. *"The dashboard is slow." "The team isn't shipping." "Users aren't activating."* That's one-third of the system. The interconnections — how the dashboard's slowness affects user trust, which affects engagement, which affects metrics, which affects which dashboards engineering prioritizes — that's where the behavior comes from. The purpose — what the system is actually optimizing for — is usually different from what the org thinks it's optimizing for.

Boundaries matter too. A system has a boundary — what's in, what's out — and the boundary is a *choice,* not a fact. Most disagreements about "what's wrong" come down to people drawing the boundary differently. *"It's a marketing problem." "No, it's a product problem."* Both might be right; they're just looking at different systems.

Purpose is the trickiest part. Meadows' line: *the purpose of a system is what it does.* Not what it says it does. Not what its founders intended. What it actually produces, reliably, over time. If your hiring process consistently produces a homogenous team, that's its purpose — even if every document about it says "diversity." Behavior reveals purpose; documentation rarely does.

## What good looks like

- A diagnosis names elements, interconnections, *and* purpose
- The boundary of the system is stated explicitly, with what's outside it that might still matter
- Purpose is derived from behavior, not from the mission statement

## What bad looks like

- "Diagnosis" that's a list of components or symptoms
- An assumed boundary that hides where the real action is
- Stated purpose (the slogan) treated as actual purpose
- Element-blame: *"if only X person/team/component would change"* — usually an interconnection problem, not an element problem

## Diagnostic questions

- What are the elements? What are the interconnections between them?
- Where's the boundary? What's outside that might still affect the inside?
- What does this system *actually do* — not what it says it does?
- If everyone in this org disappeared and only the system remained, what would it keep producing?

## Craft move

Spend more time on interconnections and purpose than on elements. Elements are the easy part. The work is in the wiring.

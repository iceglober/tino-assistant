# Ch. 8 — Seeing from inside

The hardest move.

You can analyze a system from the outside with clean detachment. You can map another company's loops, name their balancing feedback, identify their mental models. But the system you're *inside* — your team, your org, your product, your market — is much harder to see, because **you're one of the loops.** Your beliefs are part of the mental model. Your actions are inputs the system has already absorbed and adjusted around. Your sense of *what's normal* was built by the system, on the system's terms.

This is the meta-skill, and it requires moves the previous chapters don't:

### Find the outside view

Talk to someone who isn't in the system. Their disorientation when they encounter your norms is *data* — they're seeing the system fresh. Their first impressions are the most valuable, before they assimilate. New hires in week one. Customers who churned. Friends in other companies. Advisors who don't depend on your continued employment.

The norm of *"let me explain why we do it this way"* is the system protecting itself from the outside view. Resist it. Listen first.

### Look for what's missing

Systems hide their dependencies. What does this team take for granted that wouldn't exist in a different team? What works only because of one specific person or process you stopped noticing? When that person leaves, what breaks?

### Notice your reactions

When you're inside a balancing loop, your reaction to a perturbation is itself part of the loop. If you feel defensive when an idea threatens the current setup, you're feeling the loop work in real time. The discomfort is information.

### Trust anomalies

When something happens that "shouldn't" — the customer who behaves nothing like your persona, the metric that diverges from prediction, the team member who keeps surfacing the uncomfortable question — that's the most informative signal you have about the gap between your model and reality. Most orgs explain anomalies away. Don't.

### Step back deliberately

Build practices that interrupt the local view. Annual offsites, quarterly retros, post-mortems on *successes* (not just failures), inviting outsiders into design reviews. The system won't surface itself; you have to schedule the seeing.

## What good looks like

- Regular use of outside-view techniques — non-team members, comparisons to other orgs
- Practices that interrupt the system's defaults (offsites, deliberate disruption, devil's-advocate roles)
- A norm of taking anomalies seriously instead of explaining them away
- Newcomers asked what surprises them, before they assimilate

## What bad looks like

- *"We've considered that and there's a good reason for it"* applied to anything new
- Anomalies routinely explained away as noise
- Newcomers' confusion treated as their problem to resolve
- Confidence about the system with no recent outside contact

## Diagnostic questions

- When did we last get a genuine outside view of this system?
- What does this team take for granted that wouldn't be true elsewhere?
- What was the last anomaly we noticed? Did we follow it, or explain it away?
- Who in this room is most likely to surface uncomfortable observations, and do we actually listen?

## Craft move

Schedule outside contact. The system you're inside will not present itself for inspection. You have to leave it, briefly, on a recurring calendar. The discipline of the schedule is what makes the seeing happen.

# Ch. 6 — Mental models

*Your model of the system is part of the system.*

Every system is operated by people who have beliefs about how it works. Their beliefs are the **mental model.** The model determines what they pay attention to, what they intervene on, and what they explain away when intervention doesn't work.

The bracing realization: the mental model is rarely accurate. It's usually a simplified version of the real system, often shaped to fit the operator's interests or comfort rather than the truth. And critically — *the mental model isn't separate from the system; it shapes the system,* because people act on their model, not on reality.

A team that believes *"users want more features"* will build more features, even when the actual problem is feature overload. A leader who believes *"the market is the bottleneck"* will keep funding marketing, even when product is the bottleneck. The model produces interventions, which produce results, which the model interprets as confirmation (because the results are consistent with what the model expected), which deepens the model. This is a reinforcing loop made of belief, and it's why some orgs persist in wrong-headed strategies for years.

This is also why most systems are hard to change. It's not the structure that's rigid — it's that the *mental models inside* the system actively maintain the structure. Reorg the boxes; the same people apply the same beliefs to the new shape; the same dynamics emerge.

Meadows ranks mental models as one of the highest-leverage points in the entire hierarchy (Ch. 7). Changing the paradigm out of which a system arises is more powerful than changing any parameter, rule, or feedback loop within it. It's also the hardest thing to change, because the people inside the system experience their mental model as *the way things obviously are.*

## What good looks like

- Explicit surfacing of mental models — *what does the team actually believe about how this works?*
- Active search for disconfirming evidence — places the model breaks
- Distinguishing model from reality in any analysis (*"I believe X; here's the evidence"*)
- Updating models when reality contradicts them, not the other way around

## What bad looks like

- Confidence about how a system works, with no recent evidence
- Dismissing data that contradicts the prevailing model
- Treating model as fact; explaining away anomalies as noise
- Hiring people who share the existing model; treating those who don't as a poor fit

## Diagnostic questions

- What does this team / org / culture *believe* about how this system works?
- Where does the belief come from? When was it last tested against fresh evidence?
- What evidence would change the belief? Has anyone looked for it?
- Whose interests does the current model serve, and is that the same as who pays when it's wrong?

## Craft move

Any system diagnosis should produce *two* outputs — the structural diagnosis (loops, stocks, delays) and the **mental-model diagnosis** (what people inside the system believe is true). Changing one without the other rarely works for long.

# Ch. 7 — Leverage points

*Where to intervene, ranked.*

Meadows' most famous contribution is a ranked list of places to intervene in a system, ordered from least powerful to most powerful. Her unsettling observation: most interventions happen at the *bottom* of the list, where leverage is lowest. The most powerful interventions happen at the top — and are also the hardest to access, often because they require changing the very beliefs that produce the system.

The hierarchy, simplified (Meadows named 12; the deep structure is what matters):

### Lower leverage — where most effort goes

- **Parameters, numbers, thresholds.** Change a value. Adjust the price, the rate, the discount, the alert threshold. Lowest leverage. Most effort. (*"Let's A/B test the button color."*)
- **Buffer sizes.** Bigger or smaller buffers. Slightly more powerful. Usually invisible.
- **Stock and flow structure.** Physical/structural changes. Slow, expensive, durable.
- **Delays in feedback loops.** Speed up or slow down feedback. Moderately powerful but often constrained by physics.

### Middle leverage

- **Strength of balancing feedback loops.** How tightly does the thermostat respond? (Rate-limiters, governors, regulators.)
- **Strength of reinforcing feedback loops.** How fast does the snowball roll? (Network effects, viral coefficients.)
- **Information flows.** Who sees what, when. Very high leverage and usually cheap. (Make a metric public; surface a previously-hidden cost.)
- **Rules.** The formal incentives, constraints, and permissions in the system.

### Highest leverage — where almost no effort goes

- **Self-organization.** The system's capacity to change its own structure. (Empowered teams, modifiable defaults, opt-in protocols.)
- **Goals.** What the system is optimized for. The metric. The mission. (Changing the goal of a corporation changes everything below it.)
- **Paradigm.** The worldview the system arises from. (*"Software is a service" vs "software is a product."*)
- **Power to transcend paradigms.** Holding any paradigm lightly. (The rarest capacity. Usually shows up as humility.)

### The pattern

Most product orgs work on parameters (A/B tests on values) and stocks (more users, more revenue), because those feel "actionable." The highest-leverage moves — changing what the system *optimizes for,* changing what the org *believes* — are rarely on the roadmap, because they don't fit the OKR framework, because they're harder to attribute, and because they implicate the leaders who set the existing goals and paradigms.

The unsettling implication: if you spent a year on parameter tuning when the actual leverage was in changing the team's mental model or the metric being optimized, you were *busy* but made no real progress. This happens constantly.

The most actionable high-leverage move is usually **information flows.** Making a previously-hidden number visible can shift behavior dramatically without changing rules, goals, or paradigms. It's also cheap.

## What good looks like

- Conscious choice of where to intervene, with leverage ranking acknowledged
- Recognition that *"we A/B tested"* is low-leverage activity
- Higher-leverage interventions (rules, information flows, goals, paradigms) attempted, not just discussed
- Information-flow interventions used heavily — high leverage, low cost

## What bad looks like

- All intervention happens at parameter/stock level
- Higher-leverage moves dismissed as *"too hard"* or *"out of scope"*
- Goals never re-examined despite repeated failed parameter tuning
- The current paradigm assumed to be the only one possible

## Diagnostic questions

- Where on the leverage ranking is our current effort focused?
- What higher-leverage moves have we considered and rejected, and why?
- What information flows are missing? Adding visibility is the cheapest high-leverage move.
- When did we last question the system's *goals* — what it's actually optimized for?

## Craft move

Before tuning a parameter, ask if there's a higher-leverage move that's harder but more powerful. Most teams discover that a two-hour conversation about goals would have prevented a two-month A/B test campaign.

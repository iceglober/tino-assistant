# Ch. 4 — Balancing loops

The thermostat.

A balancing loop is a feedback structure where a change in one variable produces *opposing* change. The thermostat is the cleanest example: room gets cold → heat turns on → room gets warm → heat turns off → room gets cold...

Balancing loops are everywhere, and they're easy to miss because *they're working when nothing seems to be happening.* A homeostatic system is silent. A market that returns to equilibrium after a perturbation. A culture that resists change. A team that absorbs new processes and quietly continues working the old way.

The big insight: **balancing loops are what produce stability,** and stability is usually invisible to the people creating it. When something doesn't change despite your efforts, you've discovered a balancing loop. Your effort is the input; the balancing loop is what's returning the system to where it was.

This is why most change initiatives fail. There's a balancing loop you didn't account for, and it's quietly undoing your work. The org culture rebounds. The metric returns to its prior level. The new process gets re-routed around. The reorg's effects fade in 18 months.

Working with balancing loops is harder than working with reinforcing loops. You can't just push harder — pushing often *strengthens* the balancing loop in response (a culture that resists change resists harder when the change is forced). You have to find what's holding the system in equilibrium and modify *that.* This is usually a goal, a rule, or a mental model (Ch. 6), not a parameter.

Balancing loops also produce **overshoot** when combined with delays (Ch. 5). A thermostat with a long sensor delay heats the room past its setpoint before noticing, then cools it past again. Most policy oscillation in orgs is balancing loops with delays.

## What good looks like

- Identification of *why is this thing stable?* — what's actively maintaining it
- Change initiatives that account for the balancing loops they'll trigger
- Recognition that resistance is information, not opposition
- Balancing loops named explicitly where stability is desired (and protected)

## What bad looks like

- *"We just need to push harder"* — usually strengthens the balancing loop
- Surprise when a change "doesn't stick"
- Treating equilibrium as the natural state rather than as an actively maintained one
- Removing a balancing loop that was preventing something worse, then meeting the worse

## Diagnostic questions

- What's keeping this system stable? What forces are actively returning it to baseline?
- If our intervention "didn't work," what balancing loop pushed back?
- What balancing loops would our proposed change trigger?
- Where would we want to *increase* the strength of a balancing loop (preventing runaway), and where would we want to *weaken* one (allowing change)?

## Craft move

When something stubbornly doesn't change, treat it as a diagnostic clue. The balancing loop you're up against is information about what the system actually values. Often the right move isn't to push harder against the loop — it's to change the goal the loop is preserving.

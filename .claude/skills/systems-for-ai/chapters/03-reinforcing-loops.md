# Ch. 3 — Reinforcing loops

The snowball.

A reinforcing loop is a feedback structure where a change in one variable produces *more change in the same direction.* The classic example is compound interest — a little money grows into more, which grows into more, which grows into more. In products: a user invites three friends, each of whom invites three more, each of whom invites three more.

Reinforcing loops produce explosive growth or explosive decline. They're rarely linear. Most "exponential" phenomena in product — viral growth, network effects, runaway losses, death spirals, market dominance — are reinforcing loops in action.

The most important fact about reinforcing loops: **they work both directions.** A network effect that pulls users in also pushes them out when it reverses. The same loop that compounds gains compounds losses. Friendster died from the same kind of loop that built MySpace. Most product founders intuit the upside; most underestimate the symmetry.

Another important fact: reinforcing loops have **limits.** No reinforcing loop continues forever — it eventually hits a balancing loop (Ch. 4) that caps it. The question is whether the limit is far away (lots of room to grow) or near (you're about to hit a wall the loop didn't warn you about).

A subtler trap: reinforcing loops that *feel* like progress but rest on something finite. A growth loop that depends on a fixed-size addressable market. A retention loop that depends on a stock that's slowly draining. The loop runs cleanly for a long time, then suddenly doesn't. From inside, the change looks abrupt; from outside, it was inevitable.

## What good looks like

- Reinforcing loops named explicitly: *"X grows when Y grows when X grows..."*
- Loop direction (virtuous or vicious) named for both directions
- Loop limits acknowledged — what eventually caps this loop, and how close are we?
- Awareness of the finite stocks the loop draws on

## What bad looks like

- "Exponential growth" treated as inevitable rather than as a structure that can reverse
- Loop-thinking applied only to upside cases, never to downside
- Assumption that the current loop will continue forever (no reinforcing loop does)
- Investment in the loop without monitoring the underlying stocks it depends on

## Diagnostic questions

- What reinforcing loops are operating here, in either direction?
- What happens to this loop if it reverses?
- What's the limit on this loop — where does it stop being self-reinforcing?
- What stock does this loop draw on, and how close is the stock to depletion?

## Craft move

Every reinforcing loop should be named in both directions. The team building the viral-growth loop should also know what the death-spiral version looks like — because the same structure produces both, and recognizing the reversal early is what lets you act before the floor falls out.
